Overview
This semester, you will create a comprehensive data-driven narrative that combines rigorous data
analysis and visualization with compelling storytelling. Your project should transform complex
data into an accessible, engaging narrative that informs and impacts your audience, much like the
professional examples outlined below.
Project Audience: Assume the audience for your project is the general public
Project submission: (1) URL to your project website (2) URL to your github classroom repo.
What is a Data-Driven Narrative?
A data-driven narrative is more than just charts and statistics. It's a story where data serves as
both evidence and protagonist, revealing patterns, trends, or truths that might otherwise remain
hidden. The best data narratives:
Tell a human story supported by quantitative evidence
Reveal something unexpected or challenge conventional wisdom
Make complex information accessible through thoughtful visualization
Invite exploration through interactive or layered presentation

Maintain journalistic integrity while engaging readers emotionally
Data-driven journalism is the practice of using structured data, statistical analysis, and
computational methods to uncover, verify, and communicate truthful, evidence-based
accounts of newsworthy phenomena.

Topic Selection
You can choose any topic; however, it must be amenable to a data-driven narrative.
Select a topic that:
Matters to you personally and will sustain your interest over an entire semester
Has accessible data that you can actually obtain and analyze
Addresses a question you genuinely want to answer
Impacts real people or communities in meaningful ways
Allows for original insight rather than simply confirming what everyone already knows
Strong topics often emerge at the intersection of your interests, available data, and societal
relevance. Consider local issues where national data might not tell the complete story, or
emerging trends that haven't been thoroughly examined through a data lens.
Feel free to choose unconventional topics. For example, students in the past have done very
engaging projects on UFOs, avocados and the black market, Bigfoot sightings, etc.
IMPORTANT: All topics and websites created MUST be academically and professionally suitable.
Avoid any violent, prejudiced, graphic, hateful, or highly politically biased topics or content. This
doesn't mean you can't address difficult topics or issues, but it needs to be done in a professional
way. Please use common sense by keeping everything appropriate and academically relevant. If
in doubt, reach out to the professor first.
Data Collection
Data collection should be a core element of your project. It is discouraged, although not
prohibited, to use a pre-built dataset, since they are typically well-studied. The goal is to make
yourself stand out and demonstrate your unique skills. Doing a topic that 1000 other students
have also done isn't a great way to accomplish this, be creative and try to make yourself stand
out. Creating a unique, novel dataset is a great way to set yourself apart.
There is so much happening in the world at the moment, and things are happening so fast that
you very well may be the first person to deeply dive in a systematic, data-driven way into a
current relevant topic. This could be a great way to make yourself stand out.
Begin by exploring data. Browse repositories like data.gov, academic datasets, or organizational
databases. Look for datasets that spark questions: Why did this change? Who is affected? What
patterns emerge? The best projects often start with curiosity about a specific dataset rather than
attempting to find data to support a predetermined conclusion.

Remember: Your goal is not to prove what you already believe, but to discover the truth, what
the data reveals, and communicate those findings compellingly to others.
Project Components
Your final project should include:
1. A Clear Narrative Arc: Like any good story, your project needs a beginning (establishing
context and questions), middle (exploring the data and revealing findings), and end
(implications and meaning).
2. Original Data Analysis: You must work with real datasets; whether publicly available data,
data you collect yourself, or data you obtain through partnerships. Your analysis should
reveal genuine insights, not simply visualize obvious trends or conclusions.
3. Thoughtful Visualization: Choose visualization methods that clarify rather than confuse.
Each chart, map, or graphic should serve your narrative and be appropriate to your data
type and story goals.
4. Contextual Depth: Supplement your data with research, expert interviews, case studies,
or other supporting material that helps readers understand what the numbers mean in
real-world terms.
5. Professional Presentation: Your final deliverable should demonstrate production quality
appropriate for public consumption.
6. Note on AI Usage: DO NOT use AI to write the narrative. You can use it as an editor,
proofreader, or for textual refactoring, but YOU need to be the author, again in this case Ai
is NOT the author, you are.
7. Optional: Generative AI Component: Generative AI is obviously a hot topic at the
moment, and familiarity with it is a marketable skill to add to your resume. You are
encouraged to include some of this in your project, either by using it to create relevant
images or videos (if you use GenAI content, please cite the source, model, and prompt).
Another example would be incorporating audio content, e.g., using AWS Polly or
ElevenLabs to create a companion podcast or a narration option for the audience.
8. AI Usage Log: If you use AI in any way, you MUST add a section at the end or in the
appendix outlining honestly how you used AI in the project.'
9. Appendix: You MUST Include a technical appendix that outlines and summarizes any
methodological or analytical details that do not fit naturally into the main narrative (e.g.,
statistical methods, dimensionality reduction, or complex data-processing steps used to
generate figures). Assume the audience of the Appendix is other data scientists (e.g., DSAN
students), while the audience for the main text is the general public.
Required Visualization Components
Your project must include the following visualization types as minimum requirements:

ALL of the following MUST be publication quality and share a uniform theme and color
palette
Static Visualizations
At least two static graphics (charts, graphs, or plots that effectively communicate key findings
without interaction)
Examples: bar charts, line graphs, scatter plots, heatmaps, small multiples
Purpose: Provide clear, immediate insights that readers can grasp at a glance
Interactive Visualizations
At least two interactive graphics (visualizations that respond to user input through hovering,
clicking, filtering, or other interactions)
Examples: filterable charts, zoomable maps, tooltips revealing detail, animated timelines
Purpose: Allow readers to explore data at their own pace and discover personal relevance
Linked Views
At least one linked view (where interaction with one visualization triggers changes in a second,
connected visualization)
Examples: selecting a category in one chart filters or highlights data in another; clicking a
map region updates an associated time series; brushing a scatter plot updates a histogram
Purpose: Reveal relationships between different dimensions of your data
Infographic
At least one infographic (a designed composition that combines text, icons, and visual elements
to explain a concept or summarize key findings). For example, this could be included at the end
as a final summary.
Examples: process diagrams, comparison layouts, annotated illustrations, visual
explainers
Purpose: Make complex information digestible through thoughtful design
Additional Considerations (optional, but nice additions)
Dashboard: Consider adding an interactive dashboard (a coordinated view bringing together
multiple data perspectives in a single, cohesive interface)
Examples: summary panels with key metrics, filterable multi-chart displays, comparative
views
Purpose: Provide comprehensive understanding and enable exploration across multiple
data dimensions

Map-Based Visualization: Consider adding a map-based visualization (if geographic patterns
are relevant to your story)
Examples: choropleth maps, point maps, flow maps, cartograms
Purpose: Reveal spatial patterns and geographic disparities
Temporal Visualization: Consider adding a temporal visualization (if your data involves
change over time)
Examples: time series, timelines, animated sequences, before/after comparisons
Purpose: Show how situations evolved or changed

You are allowed, and encouraged, to go beyond these minimum requirements. However,
remember: visualizations should only be included if they strengthen or add to the
narrative. Avoid plotting for plotting's sake. Every graphic should earn its place by advancing
understanding, revealing patterns, or supporting your argument. Quality and purposefulness
matter far more than quantity.
Technical Considerations
Hosting Requirements
You MUST host your project online where it can be accessed and graded. Understanding
hosting requirements is crucial to your technical planning.
It is recommended to use Quarto, but not required.
Client-Side Tools (highly recommended)
What they are: Technologies that run entirely in the user's web browser (HTML, CSS,
JavaScript, D3.js, Leaflet, Chart.js, etc.)
Why recommended: Can be hosted on GU Domains, which provides free, reliable hosting
for students
Advantages: No server maintenance, no hosting costs, guaranteed availability through
semester end and beyond
Examples of client-side approaches: Static HTML pages, JavaScript visualizations,
embedded Tableau Public dashboards, Flourish embeds
Server-Side Tools (optional: requires additional planning)
What they are: Technologies requiring backend processing (Streamlit, Shiny, Flask,
Django, etc.)
Important: If you choose server-side tools, you are responsible for finding and
maintaining web hosting and server-side functionality until the project is graded
Challenges: Requires paid hosting services (AWS, Heroku, DigitalOcean, etc.), server

configuration, ongoing maintenance, potential crashes or downtime
Consider carefully: Is the server-side functionality essential to your story, or could you
achieve similar results with client-side tools?
Recommendation: Unless your project absolutely requires server-side processing (real-time data
updates, complex backend calculations, database queries), strongly consider using client-side
technologies that can be hosted on GU Domains. This ensures your work remains accessible and
eliminates technical hosting issues during the grading period.
Success Criteria
Excellent projects should demonstrate:
Analytical rigor: Sound methodology, appropriate statistical approaches, honest
representation of data
Narrative clarity: A coherent story that guides readers through complexity without
oversimplifying
Visual effectiveness: Graphics that illuminate rather than obscure, with careful attention
to design principles
Ethical responsibility: Accurate representation of data, appropriate context,
acknowledgment of limitations
Audience engagement: Content that respects readers' intelligence while remaining
accessible
Technical proficiency: Reliable hosting, functional interactivity, professional presentation
Remember: The goal is to create something you're proud to share, i.e. work that demonstrates
your skills, tells a compelling story, and contributes meaningfully to public understanding of an
important issue.
